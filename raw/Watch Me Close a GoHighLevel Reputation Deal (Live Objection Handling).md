> [!tldr]
> [[Clay Lawrence]] reacts to one of his earliest [[Review Harvest]] sales calls, where he closes a cleaning business owner at $97/month — despite breaking nearly every rule of the [[CLOSER Framework]] he now swears by. The video alternates between the raw call recording and Clay's live commentary, flagging what went wrong (no deficit established, lost control of the conversation, feature-heavy pitch) and what accidentally worked (strong differentiators, honest objection handling, and just enough persistence to close).

## The Business & the Setup (0:00 – 1:56)

[[Clay Lawrence]] runs [[Review Harvest]] (reviewharvest.com), a done-for-you service that helps local businesses collect more [[Google Meu Negócio|Google Business Profile]] reviews via automated text and email sequences. The model charges $97–$279/month depending on volume and, at time of recording, was generating over $58,000/month in largely recurring revenue with ~85 clients. This video is a reaction/review of one of his early calls — one he specifically chose because he knew it was messy.

## The Call Opens (1:56 – 6:40)

The prospect is a residential and commercial cleaning company owner with about 40 Google reviews and 50 weekly clients, looking to double that. He found Review Harvest through Instagram ads, had already spoken with a competitor ([[Soto Tellus]]) at a conference, and enters the call ready to negotiate. Clay's retrospective commentary tracks his failures against the [[CLOSER Framework]] he loosely adapts from [[Alex Hormozi]]:

- **C — Clarify** why they're there: Clay skips it. He never gets the prospect to admit a specific deficit ("my review process is broken") before pitching.
- **L — Label** them with a concern: Also skipped. The correct move would have been "sounds like you want to double your weekly cleans, and to do that you need more Google reviews — is that right?" Getting that verbal confirmation arms you for every objection that follows.
- **O — Overview** past pain: Skipped. Clay jumps straight to the pitch without building any sense of what the prospect has already tried.

> [!quote] Clay on losing control of the call
> "Whoever's asking the question is controlling the call. And so here I haven't really pushed my questions yet and now he's already pretty much presenting objections."

## Pitching the Service (6:40 – 12:30)

Clay presents three core activities — request reviews, respond to reviews, repurpose reviews — but his own retrospective critique is that these are feature labels, not outcome language. He flags the preferred reframe:

| Feature label | Outcome reframe |
|---|---|
| Request reviews | Increase awareness — rank higher on [[Google Maps Pack]] |
| Respond to reviews | Build trust — show Google and customers you're reputable |
| Repurpose reviews | Stay top of mind — convert followers on Instagram and Facebook |

Two differentiators that do land well:
1. **Personalized image per review request** — a photo of the business owner with the customer's name overlaid, sent by text and email. Breaks through generic "please leave a review" fatigue.
2. **[[Feeding America]] donation per review** — every review a client earns triggers a meal donation. Reframes the ask from a transaction to a favor with social impact.

The service is built on [[GoHighLevel]] (HighLevel), which handles the automation backbone. Clay also offers a custom [[Zapier]]-compatible integration and pre-verifies toll-free numbers so clients don't have to navigate [[Twilio]] registration themselves.

## Handling Objections (12:30 – 21:00)

The prospect raises three distinct concerns:

**"Will it be spammy?"**
Clay explains the cadence (2 texts + 3 emails for reactivation), auto-removal on any click or reply, and timing logic (send at the "aha moment" — e.g., 5–6:30 pm when a homeowner returns to find a clean house). He retrospectively notes he changed "minimize customer complaints" to "minimize bugging customers" — softer framing, same concept.

**"What about data privacy / my customers' info?"**
Review Harvest collects only name, phone, email, and appointment-end time. Data lives on HighLevel, which is [[SOC 2]] compliant. Clay notes he should have led with that certification instead of the ad-hoc explanation he gave on this call.

**"Will I get reviews from people in my city? (Chicago area)"**
The prospect worries locals aren't engaged. Clay pivots to social proof from existing Chicagoland clients — a garage coating company, a poop-scooping company, a stump grinding company — all getting results. He notes a 4–6% conversion rate even in "cold" New York markets, versus 35%+ for high-touch services like photographers.

## The Close & Retrospective (21:00 – 25:00)

Clay offers a two-week free trial, requires only a card on file to start, and walks through pricing tiers ($97 → $179 → $279 by new monthly customers). The prospect picks $97/month. The call closes at around 44 minutes — far longer than Clay's current target. His summary:

> [!quote] Clay's post-call verdict
> "This is an example of me not really clarifying the call, not dominating the conversation, not controlling it by asking questions, not clarifying why he was there, not following the process really at all — and still closed them."

The prospect became a multi-location client, generating $2,000+ in revenue before canceling ~2 years later. Clay frames it as proof that even broken process closes deals when the product is differentiated and the objections are handled honestly.
